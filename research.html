Research Statement

Proteins evolve at different rates, but the reasons why remain unclear. A common measure of evolutionary change is the number of amino acid–altering substitutions (dN), which reflects how selection shapes gene function. Many factors—such as sex-biased expression, tissue specificity, and gene expression levels—have been suggested as drivers of protein divergence, yet their relative importance is not well understood.

My research uses comparative genomics across eight Drosophila species to investigate these drivers. By integrating RNA-seq data with features such as codon usage, gene length, chromosomal location, protein–protein interaction, and GC content, I aim to build a comprehensive picture of how both regulatory and sequence-based features shape molecular evolution.

To approach this question, I calculate sex bias, tissue specificity (τ), and expression levels from RNA-seq data across tissues and species, while also incorporating sequence features like codon adaptation index (CAI), GC content, and protein interaction connectivity. Using generalized linear models (GLMs), I test how these factors predict amino acid substitution rates (dN). I also apply codon-based phylogenetic models in HyPhy to detect adaptive diversification, and the Expression Variance and Evolution (EVE) model to assess how expression divergence compares to within-species diversity.

By combining expression-based and sequence-based features, my work aims to disentangle the forces shaping protein divergence. This integrative approach will provide new insight into how gene regulation and selective pressures interact to generate molecular diversity across species.
